C5.1

The Reactivity Series

The order of metals and how they react with oxygen, water and acids

The Reactivity Series

The reactivity series is a list of metals arranged in order of their reactivity, from most reactive at the top to least reactive at the bottom. Carbon and hydrogen are included as reference points for extraction methods.

Potassium (K)
Sodium (Na)
Lithium (Li)
Calcium (Ca)
Magnesium (Mg)
— Carbon (C) —
Aluminium (Al)
Zinc (Zn)
Iron (Fe)
— Hydrogen (H) —
Copper (Cu)
Silver (Ag)
Gold (Au)
Platinum (Pt)
A common mnemonic: Please Stop Letting Careless Monkeys Carry Aluminium Zinc Iron Home Copper Silver Gold Platinum. The more reactive a metal, the more easily it loses electrons to form positive ions.

Oxidation and Reduction (in terms of oxygen)

Oxidation is the gain of oxygen.
Reduction is the loss of oxygen.

Remember: OIL RIG – Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons), Reduction Is Gain (of electrons). But at foundation level, you need to know it in terms of oxygen.

When a metal reacts with oxygen, the metal is oxidised (it gains oxygen). If a metal oxide loses its oxygen, the metal oxide is reduced.

2Mg + O2 → 2MgO

Magnesium is oxidised – it gains oxygen.

Metals Reacting with Oxygen

When metals react with oxygen, they form metal oxides. The more reactive the metal, the more vigorously it reacts.

metal + oxygen → metal oxide
Metal Word Equation Observations
Magnesium magnesium + oxygen → magnesium oxide Burns with a brilliant white flame; white powder (ash) formed
Iron iron + oxygen → iron oxide Glows orange/yellow; forms grey/black solid (iron oxide)
Copper copper + oxygen → copper oxide Surface slowly turns black (copper oxide layer forms)

Balanced Symbol Equations

2Mg(s) + O2(g) → 2MgO(s)
4Fe(s) + 3O2(g) → 2Fe2O3(s)
2Cu(s) + O2(g) → 2CuO(s)

Metals Reacting with Water

Reactive metals react with water to produce a metal hydroxide and hydrogen gas.

metal + water → metal hydroxide + hydrogen
Metal Reaction with Water Observations
Potassium Very vigorous Fizzes rapidly, moves on surface, lilac flame, melts into a ball
Sodium Vigorous Fizzes, moves on surface, melts into a silvery ball, may ignite
Lithium Steady Fizzes steadily, moves on surface, gradually dissolves
Calcium Moderate Fizzes, sinks, water turns milky/cloudy
Magnesium Very slow with cold water Very few bubbles with cold water; reacts well with steam
Iron Does not react with water No reaction (but does react very slowly with steam)
Copper No reaction No reaction at all

Example Balanced Equations

2Na(s) + 2H2O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + H2(g)
2K(s) + 2H2O(l) → 2KOH(aq) + H2(g)
Ca(s) + 2H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)

Magnesium reacts with steam (not cold water):

Mg(s) + H2O(g) → MgO(s) + H2(g)

Metals Reacting with Acids

Metals above hydrogen in the reactivity series react with dilute acids to produce a salt and hydrogen gas.

metal + acid → salt + hydrogen

The name of the salt depends on the acid used:

  • Hydrochloric acid → metal chloride
  • Sulfuric acid → metal sulfate
  • Nitric acid → metal nitrate (but nitric acid reactions are more complex)
Metal With HCl Observations
Magnesium magnesium + hydrochloric acid → magnesium chloride + hydrogen Vigorous fizzing, metal dissolves quickly, solution warms up
Zinc zinc + hydrochloric acid → zinc chloride + hydrogen Steady fizzing, metal dissolves slowly
Iron iron + hydrochloric acid → iron chloride + hydrogen Slow fizzing, very slow dissolving, solution turns pale green
Copper No reaction No fizzing, no change – copper is below hydrogen

Balanced Symbol Equations

Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl2(aq) + H2(g)
Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl2(aq) + H2(g)
Fe(s) + 2HCl(aq) → FeCl2(aq) + H2(g)
Mg(s) + H2SO4(aq) → MgSO4(aq) + H2(g)
Hydrogen gas can be tested with a burning splint – you hear a squeaky pop.

Uses of Metals and Reactivity

The reactivity of a metal determines what it can be used for:

  • Gold and platinum are used for jewellery because they are unreactive – they do not tarnish or corrode.
  • Copper is used for water pipes and electrical wiring because it is unreactive with water and is a good conductor.
  • Iron/steel is used in construction but must be painted or galvanised to prevent rusting (it reacts slowly with oxygen and water).
  • Aluminium forms a tough oxide layer that protects it from further corrosion, making it useful for aircraft and drinks cans.
Investigating the reactivity of metals with dilute acid

Method: Place equal-sized pieces of different metals into test tubes containing equal volumes of dilute hydrochloric acid at the same concentration. Observe and record the rate of fizzing.

Control variables:
  • Volume and concentration of acid
  • Size/mass of metal pieces
  • Temperature of acid
Independent variable: Type of metal
Dependent variable: Rate/vigour of reaction (volume of gas produced, or time for metal to dissolve)

Evaluating the investigation: Consider whether the metal pieces were truly the same surface area, whether the temperature was controlled, and whether subjective observations (like “vigorous”) could be made more quantitative by collecting gas in a gas syringe.

Practice Questions

Question 1 1 mark

Which of the following metals reacts most vigorously with water?

Correct!

Potassium is the most reactive metal listed. It reacts violently with water, producing a lilac flame and moving rapidly across the surface.

Question 2 1 mark

What is produced when zinc reacts with sulfuric acid?

Correct!

Metal + sulfuric acid → metal sulfate + hydrogen. So zinc + sulfuric acid → zinc sulfate + hydrogen.

Question 3 2 marks

Define the terms oxidation and reduction in terms of oxygen.

Model Answer

Oxidation is the gain of oxygen. [1 mark]
Reduction is the loss of oxygen. [1 mark]

Question 4 3 marks

A student adds a piece of magnesium ribbon to dilute hydrochloric acid. Describe what they would observe, write the word equation, and write the balanced symbol equation.

Model Answer

Observation: The magnesium fizzes vigorously / bubbles of gas are produced. The magnesium dissolves and the solution gets warm. [1 mark]

Word equation: magnesium + hydrochloric acid → magnesium chloride + hydrogen [1 mark]

Symbol equation: Mg(s) + 2HCl(aq) → MgCl2(aq) + H2(g) [1 mark]

Question 5 1 mark

Which metal does NOT react with dilute hydrochloric acid?

Correct!

Copper is below hydrogen in the reactivity series, so it cannot displace hydrogen from an acid. No reaction occurs.

Question 6 3 marks

Explain why gold is suitable for use in jewellery but iron is not, with reference to the reactivity series.

Model Answer

Gold is at the bottom of the reactivity series, meaning it is very unreactive [1 mark]. It does not react with oxygen or water, so it does not corrode or tarnish [1 mark]. Iron is higher in the reactivity series and reacts with oxygen and water to form rust (iron oxide), so it would corrode and lose its appearance [1 mark].

Question 7 4 marks

A student investigates the reactivity of metals by adding pieces of metal to dilute acid and timing how long it takes for the metal to dissolve. Suggest two control variables and explain how the student could improve the validity of their results.

Model Answer

Control variables (any two):

  • Volume of acid [1 mark]
  • Concentration of acid [1 mark]
  • Size/surface area of the metal piece [1 mark]
  • Starting temperature of the acid [1 mark]

Improving validity: The student could collect the gas produced in a gas syringe and measure the volume of gas in a given time, rather than subjectively judging the vigour of the reaction. They should also repeat each test at least 3 times and calculate a mean [1 mark].

Question 8 2 marks

Write a balanced symbol equation, including state symbols, for the reaction of calcium with water.

Model Answer
Ca(s) + 2H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)

[1 mark for correct formula and balancing, 1 mark for correct state symbols]
