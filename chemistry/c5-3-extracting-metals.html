C5.3

Extracting Metals

How metals are obtained from their ores using reduction

Metals and Ores

An ore is a rock that contains enough of a metal or metal compound to make it worthwhile extracting the metal.

Most metals are found in the Earth's crust as compounds, usually as metal oxides or metal sulfides. The method used to extract a metal depends on its position in the reactivity series.

Position in Reactivity Series Extraction Method Examples
Above carbon (very reactive) Electrolysis Potassium, sodium, lithium, calcium, aluminium
Below carbon (less reactive) Reduction with carbon (or hydrogen) Zinc, iron, copper
Very bottom (unreactive) Found uncombined (native metals) Gold, platinum

Oxidation and Reduction in Extraction

Oxidation = gain of oxygen (or loss of electrons)
Reduction = loss of oxygen (or gain of electrons)

When we extract a metal from its ore, we need to remove the oxygen from the metal oxide. This means the metal oxide is being reduced.

The substance that removes the oxygen (e.g. carbon) gains oxygen, so it is oxidised.

In any extraction reaction, identify what gains oxygen (oxidised) and what loses oxygen (reduced). The metal oxide is always reduced. The reducing agent (carbon/hydrogen) is always oxidised.

Extraction by Reduction with Carbon

Metals below carbon in the reactivity series can be extracted by heating their oxide with carbon. Carbon is more reactive than these metals, so it can “steal” the oxygen away.

Extracting Iron (Blast Furnace)

iron oxide + carbon → iron + carbon dioxide
2Fe2O3(s) + 3C(s) → 4Fe(l) + 3CO2(g)
  • Iron oxide is reduced – it loses oxygen
  • Carbon is oxidised – it gains oxygen

Extracting Zinc

zinc oxide + carbon → zinc + carbon dioxide
2ZnO(s) + C(s) → 2Zn(s) + CO2(g)

Extracting Copper

copper oxide + carbon → copper + carbon dioxide
2CuO(s) + C(s) → 2Cu(s) + CO2(g)
Carbon can only reduce metals that are below it in the reactivity series. Metals above carbon (like aluminium, even though it has a relatively low position) must be extracted by electrolysis, which is more expensive.

Extraction by Reduction with Hydrogen

Hydrogen can also be used as a reducing agent to extract metals from their oxides. When hydrogen reduces a metal oxide, water is produced instead of carbon dioxide.

copper oxide + hydrogen → copper + water
CuO(s) + H2(g) → Cu(s) + H2O(l)
  • Copper oxide is reduced – it loses oxygen
  • Hydrogen is oxidised – it gains oxygen

Using hydrogen instead of carbon avoids producing CO2 (a greenhouse gas), but hydrogen is more difficult and dangerous to handle.

Metals Found Uncombined

Some metals are so unreactive that they do not combine with other elements. They are found in the Earth's crust as pure elements (native metals).

  • Gold – found as nuggets or flakes in rocks and rivers
  • Platinum – found uncombined
  • Silver – sometimes found uncombined (though also found as compounds)

These metals are at the bottom of the reactivity series. They do not react with oxygen, water, or acids under normal conditions, which is why they have been used for coins, jewellery, and decorations throughout history.

If asked “why is gold found uncombined?” the answer is: gold is very unreactive / it is at the bottom of the reactivity series / it does not react with other elements in the Earth's crust.

Metals Above Carbon: Electrolysis

Very reactive metals like aluminium, sodium, and potassium cannot be extracted by reduction with carbon because they are more reactive than carbon.

These metals must be extracted using electrolysis (passing an electric current through the molten ore or a solution). This is more expensive because it requires a lot of electrical energy.

For example, aluminium is extracted from its ore bauxite (aluminium oxide) by electrolysis of the molten aluminium oxide.

Practice Questions

Question 1 1 mark

Which method is used to extract aluminium from its ore?

Correct!

Aluminium is above carbon in the reactivity series, so it cannot be reduced by carbon. Electrolysis of molten aluminium oxide is needed.

Question 2 3 marks

Iron is extracted from iron oxide using carbon in a blast furnace. Write the word equation and the balanced symbol equation for this reaction. Identify which substance is oxidised and which is reduced.

Model Answer

Word equation: iron oxide + carbon → iron + carbon dioxide [1 mark]

2Fe2O3 + 3C → 4Fe + 3CO2

[1 mark for balanced equation]

Iron oxide is reduced (it loses oxygen). Carbon is oxidised (it gains oxygen). [1 mark]

Question 3 1 mark

Why is gold found uncombined in the Earth's crust?

Correct!

Gold is at the very bottom of the reactivity series. It is so unreactive that it does not form compounds with other elements, so it exists as the pure element in the Earth's crust.

Question 4 3 marks

Copper oxide is heated with hydrogen gas.

  1. Write the balanced symbol equation for this reaction. [1 mark]
  2. Identify which substance is oxidised and which is reduced. [2 marks]
Model Answer

(a)

CuO(s) + H2(g) → Cu(s) + H2O(l)

(b) Copper oxide is reduced because it loses oxygen [1 mark]. Hydrogen is oxidised because it gains oxygen (to form water) [1 mark].

Question 5 3 marks

Explain why the extraction of aluminium is more expensive than the extraction of iron.

Model Answer

Aluminium is more reactive than carbon [1 mark], so it cannot be extracted by reduction with carbon like iron can [1 mark]. Instead, aluminium must be extracted by electrolysis, which requires a large amount of electrical energy and is therefore more expensive [1 mark].

Question 6 1 mark

In the reaction: ZnO + C → Zn + CO2, what is the reducing agent?

Correct!

Carbon is the reducing agent because it causes the zinc oxide to be reduced (lose its oxygen). The carbon itself is oxidised in the process.

Question 7 2 marks

A student suggests that sodium could be extracted from sodium oxide by heating it with carbon. Explain why this would not work.

Model Answer

Sodium is more reactive than carbon [1 mark]. Carbon can only reduce the oxides of metals that are less reactive than carbon, so it cannot remove the oxygen from sodium oxide [1 mark].
