C4.2

Equations & Calculations

Balancing equations, conservation of mass, Mr, moles, and calculating masses

Conservation of Mass

The law of conservation of mass states that no atoms are created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. The total mass of the products equals the total mass of the reactants.

This is because during a chemical reaction, the atoms are simply rearranged into new substances. The same atoms are present before and after the reaction – just in different combinations.

This is the fundamental reason why chemical equations must be balanced – the same number of each type of atom must appear on both sides of the equation.

Explaining Mass Changes in Experiments

Sometimes in experiments, the measured mass appears to change. This doesn't mean mass has been created or destroyed – it means a gas has escaped or been absorbed.

Mass Appears to Decrease

When a metal reacts with acid in an open container:

  • Hydrogen gas is produced and escapes into the air
  • The mass of the container and contents decreases
  • The “lost” mass is the mass of the hydrogen gas that escaped

Similarly, when a carbonate decomposes:

calcium carbonate → calcium oxide + carbon dioxide

CO2 gas escapes, so the mass of the solid remaining is less than the mass of the starting material.

Mass Appears to Increase

When a metal burns in air (e.g. magnesium):

  • The metal reacts with oxygen from the air
  • The oxygen adds to the metal, forming a metal oxide
  • The mass of the product is greater than the mass of the starting metal
  • The “extra” mass comes from the oxygen in the air
magnesium + oxygen → magnesium oxide

Mass of MgO > mass of Mg because oxygen atoms have been added.

In a closed system (sealed container), the total mass will always stay the same. Mass only appears to change when gases can enter or leave the container.

Why Chemical Equations Must Be Balanced

Chemical equations must be balanced because of the law of conservation of mass. Atoms cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction, so:

  • The number of atoms of each element on the left side (reactants) must equal the number on the right side (products)
  • We balance equations by putting numbers in front of formulae (coefficients), never by changing the formulae themselves

How to Balance an Equation

Example: Balance the equation for magnesium reacting with hydrochloric acid.

Step 1: Write the unbalanced equation:

Mg + HCl → MgCl2 + H2

Step 2: Count atoms on each side:

ElementLeftRightBalanced?
Mg11Yes
H12No
Cl12No

Step 3: Put a 2 in front of HCl:

Mg + 2HCl → MgCl2 + H2

Step 4: Re-check:

ElementLeftRightBalanced?
Mg11Yes
H22Yes
Cl22Yes

The equation is now balanced.

Interpreting Equations in Terms of Mole Ratios H

The coefficients (big numbers) in a balanced equation tell us the ratio of moles of each substance that react or are produced.

Example:

2Mg + O2 → 2MgO

This tells us:

  • 2 moles of magnesium react with 1 mole of oxygen to produce 2 moles of magnesium oxide
  • The mole ratio is 2 : 1 : 2

More Examples

Equation Mole Ratio
Mg + 2HCl → MgCl2 + H2 1 : 2 : 1 : 1
2Na + 2H2O → 2NaOH + H2 2 : 2 : 2 : 1
2Fe2O3 + 3C → 4Fe + 3CO2 2 : 3 : 4 : 3
If a substance has no number in front of it, the coefficient is 1. For example, in Fe2O3 + 3C → 2Fe + 3CO2, there is 1 mole of Fe2O3.

Relative Formula Mass (Mr)

The relative formula mass (Mr) of a substance is the sum of the relative atomic masses (Ar) of all the atoms in its formula.

You can find relative atomic masses on the periodic table. Some key values to know:

ElementSymbolAr
HydrogenH1
CarbonC12
NitrogenN14
OxygenO16
SodiumNa23
MagnesiumMg24
SulfurS32
ChlorineCl35.5
CalciumCa40
IronFe56
CopperCu64
ZincZn65

Worked Examples

Example 1: Calculate the Mr of water (H2O):

Mr = (2 × 1) + 16 = 18

Example 2: Calculate the Mr of magnesium oxide (MgO):

Mr = 24 + 16 = 40

Example 3: Calculate the Mr of calcium carbonate (CaCO3):

Mr = 40 + 12 + (3 × 16) = 40 + 12 + 48 = 100

Example 4: Calculate the Mr of iron(III) oxide (Fe2O3):

Mr = (2 × 56) + (3 × 16) = 112 + 48 = 160
Common mistake: Watch out for brackets in formulae. In Ca(OH)2, the subscript 2 applies to everything inside the brackets: Mr = 40 + 2 × (16 + 1) = 40 + 34 = 74.

The Mole and Moles Calculations H

One mole of a substance contains the same number of particles (atoms, molecules, or formula units). This number is called the Avogadro constant and is approximately 6.02 × 1023 per mole.

The mass of one mole of a substance (in grams) is equal to its relative formula mass. For example:

  • 1 mole of carbon (Ar = 12) has a mass of 12 g
  • 1 mole of water (Mr = 18) has a mass of 18 g
  • 1 mole of CaCO3 (Mr = 100) has a mass of 100 g

The Moles Formula

moles = mass (g) ÷ Mr

This can be rearranged:

  • mass = moles × Mr
  • Mr = mass ÷ moles

Worked Examples

Example 1: How many moles are in 48 g of magnesium (Ar = 24)?

moles = 48 ÷ 24 = 2 mol

Example 2: What is the mass of 0.5 moles of calcium carbonate (Mr = 100)?

mass = 0.5 × 100 = 50 g

Example 3: How many moles are in 11.2 g of iron (Ar = 56)?

moles = 11.2 ÷ 56 = 0.2 mol
Always show your working in exam calculations. Even if your final answer is wrong, you can pick up method marks for using the correct formula and substitution.

Calculating Masses from Balanced Equations H

You can use a balanced equation and mole calculations to work out the mass of a product formed or a reactant needed.

Step-by-Step Method

  1. Write the balanced equation
  2. Calculate the moles of the substance you know (using moles = mass ÷ Mr)
  3. Use the mole ratio from the equation to find moles of the substance you want
  4. Calculate the mass (using mass = moles × Mr)

Worked Example 1

Question: What mass of magnesium oxide is produced when 6 g of magnesium burns in excess oxygen?

Step 1: Balanced equation:

2Mg + O2 → 2MgO

Step 2: Calculate moles of Mg (Ar = 24):

moles of Mg = 6 ÷ 24 = 0.25 mol

Step 3: Use mole ratio – from the equation, 2 mol Mg produces 2 mol MgO, so the ratio is 1 : 1:

moles of MgO = 0.25 mol

Step 4: Calculate mass of MgO (Mr = 24 + 16 = 40):

mass = 0.25 × 40 = 10 g

Worked Example 2

Question: What mass of iron can be extracted from 320 g of iron(III) oxide using excess carbon?

Step 1: Balanced equation:

2Fe2O3 + 3C → 4Fe + 3CO2

Step 2: Calculate moles of Fe2O3 (Mr = (2 × 56) + (3 × 16) = 160):

moles of Fe2O3 = 320 ÷ 160 = 2 mol

Step 3: Use mole ratio – from the equation, 2 mol Fe2O3 produces 4 mol Fe:

moles of Fe = 4 mol

Step 4: Calculate mass of Fe (Ar = 56):

mass = 4 × 56 = 224 g
Common mistake: Students sometimes use the Mr of the wrong substance. Always check: use the Mr of the substance whose mass you know in step 2, and the Mr of the substance whose mass you want in step 4.

Practice Questions

Question 1 1 mark

When magnesium burns in air, the mass of the product (magnesium oxide) is greater than the mass of the magnesium. Why?

Correct!

Magnesium reacts with oxygen from the air. The oxygen atoms add to the magnesium, so the product (MgO) has a greater mass than the magnesium alone. Mass is conserved – the “extra” mass came from the oxygen in the air.

Question 2 2 marks

A student reacts zinc with hydrochloric acid in an open beaker. They notice the mass of the beaker and contents decreases during the reaction. Explain why.

Model Answer

Hydrogen gas is produced during the reaction [1 mark]. The hydrogen gas escapes from the open beaker into the air, so the total mass of the beaker and contents decreases [1 mark]. (The total mass of all products, including the escaped gas, still equals the mass of the reactants – conservation of mass.)

Question 3 1 mark

What is the coefficient needed in front of HCl to balance this equation?
Zn + ?HCl → ZnCl2 + H2

Correct!

ZnCl2 has 2 chlorine atoms on the right, and H2 has 2 hydrogen atoms. So we need 2HCl on the left to provide 2 H atoms and 2 Cl atoms. The balanced equation is: Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl2 + H2

Question 4 2 marks

Balance the following equations:

  1. Na + H2O → NaOH + H2
  2. Fe + O2 → Fe2O3
Model Answer

(a)

2Na + 2H2O → 2NaOH + H2

[1 mark]

(b)

4Fe + 3O2 → 2Fe2O3

[1 mark]

Question 5 H 1 mark

In the equation: 2Mg + O2 → 2MgO, what is the mole ratio of magnesium to oxygen to magnesium oxide?

Correct!

The coefficients tell us the mole ratio: 2 moles of Mg react with 1 mole of O2 to form 2 moles of MgO. The ratio is 2 : 1 : 2.

Question 6 2 marks

State the law of conservation of mass and explain why chemical equations must be balanced.

Model Answer

The law of conservation of mass states that no atoms are created or destroyed in a chemical reaction [1 mark]. Chemical equations must be balanced so that the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides of the equation, reflecting the fact that atoms are only rearranged, not created or destroyed [1 mark].

Question 7 H 2 marks

For the reaction: Fe2O3 + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO2

  1. How many moles of CO are needed to react with 1 mole of Fe2O3? [1 mark]
  2. How many moles of Fe are produced from 2 moles of Fe2O3? [1 mark]
Model Answer

(a) 3 moles of CO [1 mark] – from the coefficients, the ratio of Fe2O3 : CO is 1 : 3.

(b) 4 moles of Fe [1 mark] – from the coefficients, the ratio of Fe2O3 : Fe is 1 : 2, so 2 moles of Fe2O3 produces 2 × 2 = 4 moles of Fe.

Question 8 1 mark

What is the relative formula mass (Mr) of calcium hydroxide, Ca(OH)2?
(Ar: Ca = 40, O = 16, H = 1)

Correct!

Ca(OH)2: Ca = 40, plus 2 × (O + H) = 2 × (16 + 1) = 34. Total Mr = 40 + 34 = 74.

Question 9 2 marks

Calculate the relative formula mass of magnesium sulfate, MgSO4.
(Ar: Mg = 24, S = 32, O = 16)

Model Answer

Mr = 24 + 32 + (4 × 16) [1 mark]

Mr = 24 + 32 + 64 = 120 [1 mark]

Question 10 H 2 marks

Calculate the number of moles in 5.6 g of iron. (Ar of Fe = 56)

Model Answer

moles = mass ÷ Mr [1 mark]

moles = 5.6 ÷ 56 = 0.1 mol [1 mark]

Question 11 H 4 marks

Zinc reacts with hydrochloric acid:
Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl2 + H2
Calculate the mass of zinc chloride produced when 13 g of zinc reacts with excess hydrochloric acid.
(Ar: Zn = 65, Cl = 35.5)

Model Answer

Step 1: Moles of Zn = 13 ÷ 65 = 0.2 mol [1 mark]

Step 2: From the equation, 1 mol Zn produces 1 mol ZnCl2, so moles of ZnCl2 = 0.2 mol [1 mark]

Step 3: Mr of ZnCl2 = 65 + (2 × 35.5) = 136 [1 mark]

Step 4: Mass = 0.2 × 136 = 27.2 g [1 mark]

Question 12 H 1 mark

What is the mass of 0.25 moles of copper oxide (CuO)?
(Ar: Cu = 64, O = 16)

Correct!

Mr of CuO = 64 + 16 = 80. Mass = moles × Mr = 0.25 × 80 = 20 g.
